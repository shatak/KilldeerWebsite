Killdeer

Charadrius vociferus

Overview

The killdeer (Charadrius vociferus) is a large plover found in the Americas. Its common name comes from its call.

Killdeers are mostly brown with rufous fringes, their heads have patches of white and black, and two black bands cross the breast. The belly and the rest of the breast are white. They can be found in coastal wetlands, beach habitats, and coastal fields.

It primarily feeds on insects along with some other invertebrates and seeds. It is a forager and prefers to look for food in areas with short vegetation and with cattle and standing water. It feeds during the day but in nonbreeding season it feeds at night during the full moon.

Description

An adult killdeer can range from 20 -28cm and have a wingspan between 59 and 63cm. They usually weigh around 72 to 121g. It has a short, thick, and dark bill, flesh-colored legs, and a red eye ring. The killdeer has a maximum lifespan of at least 10 years and 11 months.

Its upperparts are mostly brown with rufous fringes, its cap, back, and wings being the former color. It has a white forehead and a white stripe behind the eye, and its lores and the upper borders to the white forehead are black. The breast and belly are white and has two black breast bands. It is the only plover in North America with two breast bands. It has a red rump with a brown tail.

The killdeer is a vocal species, calling even at night. Its calls include nasal notes, like "deee", "tyeeee", and "kil-deee" (the basis of its common name). During display flights, it repeats a call of "kil-deer" or "kee-deeyu". When this plover is disturbed, it emits notes in a rapid sequence, such as "kee-di-di-di". Its alarm call is a long, fast trill.

Nesting and young

The killdeer nests in open fields or other flat areas with short vegetation such as agricultural fields and meadows. Their nest consist of a shallow depression or scrape in the ground lined with some stones and grass. Killdeer lays four to six eggs at a time. The female produces eggs while the male defends his territory. The eggs are approximately 38 by 27mm in size and are beige with brown markings and black speckles.

The eggs are typically laid from mid-March to early June in the southern portion of the range, and from mid-April to mid-July in the northern part.

About 53% of eggs are lost, primarily due to predators. Those that survive are closely always guarded by at least one parent. The young are brooded during rain until about 15 days after hatching and during night for about 18 days after hatching. The only time when they are not in the presence of a parent is when the parents are mating or responding to a predator.

Their young fledge about 31 days after hatching, and generally move to moister areas in valleys and on the banks of rivers. They may be cared for by their parents for up to 10 days after they fledge, and exceptionally for 81 days after hatching.

Diet

Its diet consists of beetles, flies, millipedes, worms, snails, spiders and some seeds. They also indulge in tree frogs and dead minnows. They are foragers by behavior and can also forage during the night according to the lunar cycle.

They use techniques like "foot-trembling", where it stands on one foot, shaking the other in shallow water for about five seconds, pecking at any prey stirred up to forage for food. They often follow plows to eat the disturbed earthworms that come to the surface.

Food chain

The killdeer is parasitized by acanthocephalans, cestodes, nematodes, and trematodes. Their common predators are herring gulls, common crows, raccoons, and striped skunks.

Killdeer use a variety of responses to distract predators during the breeding season. They used tactics like the “broken – wing display” to distract the predator from the nest.

Another method is the "ungulate display", where the adult raises its wings, exposes its rump, lowers its head, and charges at the intruder. This can be fatal to the displaying bird.

Conservation

The killdeer is considered a least-concern species by the IUCN due to its large range of about 26.3 million km2 (10.2 million sq mi) and population, estimated by the IUCN to be about one million birds, or about two million, according to the Handbook of the Birds of the World Alive.

Though the population is declining, it is not decreasing fast enough to be considered a vulnerable species. It is protected in the US by the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, and in Canada by the Migratory Birds Convention
